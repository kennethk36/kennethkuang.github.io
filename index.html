Project 01

Computer Vision Capstone

Data science capstone applying YOLOv8 to aerial vehicle detection — three controlled experiments across confidence threshold, IoU threshold, and image size, plus live drone footage tests at 150ft, 250ft, and 350ft to find the highest viable detection altitude.

YOLOv8 Computer Vision Python COCO Dataset Deep Learning

Goals

  • Goal 1 — Understand inference tuning: systematically study how confidence threshold, IoU threshold, and input image size affect real-world detection output — not just make it run, but know exactly which parameters to pull and why
  • Goal 2 — Max drone altitude: determine how high a DJI drone can fly above a parking lot before detection breaks down, and whether tuning those parameters can push that ceiling higher

Dataset

  • Flew a DJI drone over a parking lot at 150ft, 250ft, and 350ft to capture real aerial footage — primary test environment for evaluating detection performance at increasing altitude
  • COCO val2017 — 5,000 images, 36,781 annotations across 80 categories (31% small, 35% medium, 34% large objects). Pre-annotated bounding boxes meant no manual labeling — focus stayed on model behavior and tuning
  • Person appears 11,000+ times in COCO; rare classes like toaster appear only 9 times — the model's confidence reflects that training distribution

How YOLOv8 Works

  • Backbone (the eyes): scans the image at three zoom levels to capture features at different scales
  • Neck (the brain): merges all three scale maps into a single unified feature representation
  • Head (the decision maker): outputs bounding box coordinates and class label in one forward pass — no separate region proposal step, which is why YOLO is fast enough for real-time video

Exp 1 — Confidence Threshold

Swept confidence from 0.05 to 0.75 on a 52-object image. At conf=0.05, the model returned 86 boxes — 34 false positives. At conf=0.75, it returned 0 — missed every object. Too low floods the output with noise; too high and real detections disappear.

  • Best setting: 0.25–0.35 depending on scene clutter — recovers real detections without flooding the system with false positives

Exp 2 — IoU Threshold

IoU (Intersection over Union) controls when overlapping boxes get merged into one. Swept from 0.10 to 0.80 — detection count stayed relatively flat (18–23 boxes) across the range, but box behavior changed meaningfully. A low threshold aggressively merges nearby detections into one clean box; a high threshold allows duplicate boxes to survive.

  • Lower IoU is better for single-object scenarios — aggressive merging produces one clean box per target rather than a cluster of overlapping detections

Exp 3 — Input Image Size

Tested on a frame with 32 small ground-truth objects. Larger input resolution lets the model see more detail — critical for small or distant targets:

  • 320px → 15 found, 82ms inference
  • 480px → 21 found, 116ms
  • 640px → 32 found, 106ms  (minimum viable for aerial footage)
  • 960px → 34 found, 180ms

Dropping to 320px missed half the targets. 640 is the floor for aerial use; step up to 960 if the hardware budget allows the extra 74ms.

Drone Altitude Experiments

Filmed the same parking lot at 150ft, 250ft, and 350ft (FAA ceiling: 400ft). Default settings work well at 150ft — at higher altitudes, lowering confidence and raising image size recovers most of the missed detections:

  • 150ft — conf=0.35, imgsz=640: very reliable at baseline, no tuning needed
  • 250ft — conf=0.20, imgsz=960: recovered most missed detections vs. default settings
  • 350ft — conf=0.15, imgsz=1280: improved significantly, still a viable operating altitude

350ft is the practical ceiling. Camera angle matters too — top-down detects far better than angled shots, since COCO was trained on ground-level photos not aerial perspectives.

Takeaway

The real output wasn't a detection pipeline — it was a clear mental model of which inference parameters matter, when to pull them, and what the tradeoffs cost. Confidence and image size have the biggest practical impact; IoU matters most when scene density is high. Those findings directly inform how to deploy detection in any real-world autonomous system.

Project 02

FPV Drone

Designed and 3D-printed a custom FPV drone frame from scratch in Fusion 360 — three full structural iterations, a mid-build electronics pivot, and a first flight on V3.

Fusion 360 3D Printing Structural Analysis Embedded Systems

Problem / Goal

Design, fabricate, and fly a fully custom FPV drone — no kit frame, no template. Every structural decision had to be made from scratch: arm geometry, motor spacing, wire routing, and electronics integration. The constraint was the school's 3D printer, which only stocked PLA — a denser, more brittle material than the carbon fiber or nylon typically used in drone frames — meaning weight and stiffness had to be carefully managed through geometry rather than material selection.

Structural Engineering

The core structural challenge was the drone's motor arms, which behave as cantilevered beams under thrust load. The governing equation for max deflection of a cantilever is:

δ_max = PL³ / (3EI)

  • P — thrust load per motor arm (force from propeller under full throttle)
  • L — arm length from root to motor mount (fixed by motor spacing requirements)
  • E — Young's modulus of PLA (~3.5 GPa), a material constant we couldn't change
  • I — second moment of area of the arm cross-section — the one variable fully under our control

Since L and E were effectively fixed, reducing deflection meant maximizing I — the cross-sectional moment of inertia. For a rectangular cross-section, I = bh³/12, meaning arm height h has a cubic effect on stiffness. V3 increased arm height significantly over V2, which eliminated the flex and vibration that had made V2 unflyable.

Iterations

V1 — Prop Clearance & Wire Routing

First iteration was too compact — arms were too short and the frame had no internal channels for wire routing. Propellers clipped the frame under load. No wire management meant a tangled build that was impossible to troubleshoot. Grounded before first flight attempt.

V2 — Structural Flex & Vibration

Extended the arms for proper prop clearance and added wire routing channels. However, the arms were designed too thin to reduce weight — the small cross-sectional height meant I was low, making deflection under thrust unacceptably high. At full throttle the arms would flex and vibrate, feeding oscillations back into the flight controller and making stable flight impossible.

V3 — Structural Fix & First Flight

Increased arm cross-sectional height to raise I, reducing deflection per the beam equation. Added internal wire routing and redesigned the central plate for cleaner component mounting. The increased stiffness eliminated vibration and the flight controller stabilized immediately. V3 achieved first successful flight.

Electronics & Hardware

  • Originally wired with 4 individual ESCs — discovered mid-build that the flight controller's signal protocol was incompatible with the individual ESC firmware. Pivoted to a 4-in-1 ESC stack, which solved the compatibility issue and consolidated the wiring significantly
  • Rerouted all power and signal wires cleanly through the frame channels — this turned out to be critical for troubleshooting across iterations, as messy wiring had hidden connection failures in V1
  • Full component stack: flight controller (FC), 4-in-1 ESC, brushless motors, LiPo battery, FPV camera, and video transmitter (VTX)
  • FPV video chain: the camera feeds a live analog signal directly into the VTX, which broadcasts on a 5.8 GHz frequency to a set of FPV goggles — giving a real-time first-person view of the flight with near-zero latency. The VTX also connects to the FC, which can inject on-screen display (OSD) data — battery voltage, throttle %, and orientation — overlaid on the video feed so you can monitor telemetry without looking away from the goggles

Limitations & Constraints

  • PLA-only constraint from the school printer meant working around ~1.24 g/cm³ density — significantly heavier than carbon fiber or nylon alternatives used in most performance FPV builds
  • Flight time limited to ~3 minutes due to the weight penalty of the PLA frame on the battery

Results

V3 achieved stable, controllable first flight with ~3 minutes flight time. The iteration process produced clear, engineering-justified improvements at each step — from geometry fixes, to stiffness analysis, to a full electronics pivot. A future iteration would use a lighter material (nylon or carbon fiber) to extend flight time and reduce structural mass budget.

Project 03

Turret Defense System

Autonomous face-tracking turret using OpenCV and a pan/tilt servo system — detects and tracks faces in real time, with a laser locked to the target centroid and an Arduino closing the control loop.

OpenCV Arduino Servo Control Computer Vision Python

Problem / Goal

Build a fully autonomous turret that detects a human face, tracks it in real time, and keeps a laser locked on target — no human input once running. The challenge was closing the full loop from camera frame to physical servo movement fast enough to feel responsive, using only a webcam, an Arduino, and two servo motors.

Hardware

  • Pan/tilt bracket with two metal gear servo motors — metal gears chosen over plastic to handle the torque load of the camera + laser assembly without stripping
  • Arduino receives angle commands over serial and writes PWM signals to each servo using the Servo library
  • Laser diode mounted rigidly to the camera assembly so both point in the same direction — no separate alignment needed
  • Python script on the host machine runs OpenCV, computes corrections, and sends commands to the Arduino at each frame

System Architecture

Three coupled layers — perception, control, and actuation — running in a continuous loop:

  • Perception: webcam feed processed by OpenCV Haar Cascade — tries frontal face first, falls back to profile cascade (and mirrored profile) so tracking survives head turns. Requires 2 consecutive detections before locking on, filtering out single-frame false positives
  • Control: face centroid is smoothed with an exponential weighted moving average (α=0.25) before being fed into the PD controller — raw pixel coordinates jump frame-to-frame, smoothing stabilizes the servo commands
  • Actuation: two metal gear servo motors on a pan/tilt bracket physically move the camera and laser assembly to chase the target. Arduino receives angle + laser state over serial each frame and writes PWM to each servo

Targeting Math

The targeting loop runs a PD controller (Kp=0.07, Kd=0.005, Ki=0) independently on pan and tilt axes. Each frame:

error_x = smooth_face_x − frame_center_x
error_y = smooth_face_y − frame_center_y

pan_delta = Kp·error_x + Kd·d(error_x)/dt
tilt_delta = Kp·error_y + Kd·d(error_y)/dt

angle_pan = clip(angle_pan + pan_delta, 0°, 180°)
angle_tilt = clip(angle_tilt + tilt_delta, 0°, 180°)

  • Kp = 0.07 — proportional gain, drives the servo toward the target; tuned to balance tracking speed against overshoot
  • Kd = 0.005 — derivative term damps oscillation by braking as the error shrinks, replacing the need for integral windup (Ki = 0)
  • Dead zone of ±40px around frame center — if the error is inside this window, no correction is sent and the PID resets to prevent servo hunting when locked on
  • Serial protocol sends <pan, tilt, laser, status> to Arduino each frame — status encodes no-face / tracking / locked so the Arduino can drive indicator LEDs independently
  • When no face is detected servos drift back toward center (2% blend per frame) and the laser can be toggled independently via keyboard

Tracking Demo

The system reliably detects and tracks faces at close range, keeping the laser locked on target as the subject moves. The full detection-to-actuation loop runs in real time.

Challenges

  • Haar Cascade detection accuracy degrades significantly at distance — face resolution drops below the detector's minimum size, causing detection to drop out. A deep learning based detector (like a lightweight YOLO model) would extend the effective tracking range
  • Serial communication latency between the Python script and Arduino added lag to the control loop — partially mitigated by reducing frame resolution on the detection pass
  • PD tuning required careful balancing — Kp too high caused oscillation, Kd too high over-damped and made tracking sluggish. Final values (Kp=0.07, Kd=0.005) were found by testing on a live target; a structured Ziegler–Nichols sweep would formalize this next time
